Daily routine

Own every hour of your working day

This schedule is your system. Follow it consistently and the CEO will trust you completely.

8:00 AM
Morning kickoff — 15 minutes

Brief both external people. Ask each person: "What is your plan for today?" Write it down immediately — this becomes the Planned column in tonight's report. Set a clear expectation: send me your update by 5 PM.

9:00 AM
On-site with the networking engineer

Watch every step. Take photos. Note what tools they use, what config they apply, what cable goes where. Ask questions — "Why did you do it this way?" Learn while you document. This builds your technical knowledge every single day.

12:30 PM
Mid-day sales check-in

Quick call or WhatsApp with the sales manager. Ask: any meetings done this morning? New leads? Note it down. This mid-day check means you catch updates without waiting until 5 PM and you stay ahead of the report.

1:00 PM
Afternoon observation + documentation

Continue on-site. Keep live notes in a notebook or your phone. For every device configured — get a screenshot. For every cable run — note the port number and destination. Build the live document as you go. Do not rely on memory at the end of the day.

4:30 PM
Collect end-of-day updates

Receive the daily log from both people. Engineer sends: tasks done + photos. Sales manager sends: calls + pipeline update. If anyone has not sent by 4:30 PM — follow up immediately. Do not let missing data slide. It looks bad in your report and sets a weak standard.

5:00 PM
Write and send the daily report

Fill in the daily template. Add your own observations and quality check. Attach 2–3 photos of today's work. Keep it professional and factual. Send to the CEO by 6 PM at the latest — every single working day, without exception.

Evening
Build your personal knowledge log

Write what YOU learned today. Keep a private notebook: what new thing did you learn from the engineer? What command did they run? What sales technique did the manager apply? This is what turns probation into a permanent role — demonstrable, compounding growth.

Probation strategy

Turn 90 days into a permanent contract

Reliability converts a probation. Brilliance is a bonus. Here is the honest plan.

01

Always send the report — even when news is bad

The CEO does not expect everything to be perfect. They expect YOU to be reliable. If the engineer was late — report it. If a task slipped — report it with a reason. A CEO trusts someone who tells the truth, not someone who hides problems.

02

Add one recommendation every day

At the end of each report write one forward-looking suggestion: "I suggest ordering CAT6 termination tools by Thursday or it will delay cabling next week." CEOs promote people who think ahead — not just people who report what already happened.

03

Build a shared Google Sheets project tracker

Create a live tracker: rows = tasks, columns = assigned person, status, due date, completed date. Share the link with the CEO on Day 1. Now they can check any time — and they will see you are organized before you even send your first report.

04

Document everything with photos

Create a Google Drive folder called "Project Progress Photos." Add 5 photos every day of physical work done. At the end of month 1 send the CEO a before/after comparison. Visual proof of progress is something words alone cannot replace.

05

Learn one technical thing per day from the engineer

Ask the engineer to explain one concept each day — VLAN configuration, how PoE works, how to read a patch panel. Write it in your private log. After 30 days you know 30 new technical topics. This makes your questions sharper and earns genuine respect.

06

Ask the CEO for feedback at end of week 2

Send a short message: "Is the format and detail of my daily reports working well for you? Anything you would like me to add or change?" This shows maturity. Most people in probation never ask this. It will stand out immediately.

07

Never skip a report — not even one day

Even on a quiet day send a short update: "Today was primarily planning and coordination. No major issues. On track for tomorrow." One missed report creates doubt. Consistency over 60–90 days is what converts a probation period into a contract.

08

End of month: present a meeting — not just a report

At the end of month 1, ask the CEO for 20 minutes to walk them through the monthly report in person. Show the before/after photos. Talk through the sales pipeline. Explain what next month looks like. This one meeting does more for your permanent position than 30 days of emails alone.
